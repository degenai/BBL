# Trunks

Half-Saiyan / half-Earthling son of Vegeta and Bulma, introduced in *Dragon Ball Z* (Akira Toriyama, 1991) as the time-traveling warrior from the Cell-saga-future timeline. Across three decades of Dragon Ball franchise output the character has been re-instantiated into four distinct named incarnations whose visual identifiers, canonical age, costume, and continuity-strand are designer-coordinated to be told apart: **Future Trunks** (Z-canon, the original; lavender-blue hair, blue gi or dark vest, Brave Sword across most of the Cell-future-timeline arc; reframed in Dragon Ball Super's Future Trunks Saga, 2016, with a red scarf and short blue-tinted hair); **Kid Trunks** (the present-timeline child Trunks raised on Earth by Bulma and Vegeta, canonically co-existing with adult Future Trunks during *Dragon Ball Z*'s Cell Saga; the Buu-Saga Junior Tournament cohort partner with Son Goten); **GT Trunks** (Toei's Dragon Ball GT continuation, 1996-1997; adult Capsule Corporation president, purple hair retained per Toei's original coloring, brown open jacket over blue gi, pulled by Goku and Pan into the Black Star Dragon Ball recovery quest); and **Trunks: Xeno** (Bandai's Xenoverse and Super Dragon Ball Heroes continuity, 2015 onward; the Time Patrol lieutenant recruited by Chronoa, Supreme Kai of Time, after the Cell defeat in his own timeline — a non-Toriyama-canon expansion that the Dragon Ball Super Card Game has heavily anchored its Xeno-faction roster around).

The three incarnations share a single canonical character (the half-Saiyan / half-Earthling lineage; sword as canonical weapon; the moral-compass-as-foil-to-Vegeta narrative role; the *future-tense-as-personal-stakes* operating mode that Toriyama installed in 1991 and that has propagated across every subsequent Trunks-anchored arc). They differ in age, costume, continuity-strand, and the *cosmological scope* of their respective stories: Future Trunks fights to save his timeline; GT Trunks helps recover the Black Star Dragon Balls to save the planet; Trunks: Xeno fights across multiple timelines to defend canonical history from the Time Breakers. The Dragon Ball Super Card Game's print history treats the three incarnations as distinguishable via the official "character tag" field in Bandai's card database (`Trunks` for the GT-era continuation, `Trunks: Future` for the Z / DBS Future Trunks Saga continuity, `Trunks: Xeno` for the Xenoverse / Super Dragon Ball Heroes incarnation), making per-card incarnation identification a clerical lookup rather than a vision-confidence problem.

## What this node anchors

Card art that depicts Trunks himself across any of the three named incarnations, the Brave Sword that operates as his canonical weapon-and-visual-signature across Future-and-Xeno phases, the Super Saiyan / SS2 transformation arcs, and the canonical cross-incarnation features (half-Saiyan lineage; mother-Bulma / father-Vegeta family relations; sword-and-ki-combat operating mode; moral-compass-to-Vegeta foil role). The character is large enough that he splits into substantively different aspects across his printings, and the BBL graph can edge those aspects together through this anchor node rather than card-to-card. The Tamiyo-precedent applies: one canonical character, multiple aspects, single anchor node that holds them all and surfaces the aspect-specific information per-card.

## The functional canon

Trunks' most BBL-relevant property is that he is the franchise's **first major time-displaced hero**, introduced in *Dragon Ball Z* (1991) as the time-traveling warrior from the Cell-saga-future timeline whose existence canonized *future-tense-as-personal-stakes* into Toriyama's storytelling toolkit. Every subsequent Dragon Ball time-travel beat (the Future Trunks Saga of *Dragon Ball Super*, 2016; the Time Patrol / Time Breakers cosmology of *Xenoverse* and *Super Dragon Ball Heroes*; the multiverse-history-as-battleground premise underlying [[xenoverse-villains]]) extends from the conceptual template Future Trunks established in 1991. The three Trunks incarnations together canonicalize the franchise's three primary timeline-relations: Future Trunks fights to *prevent* a future-becoming-present (the bad-future-timeline he came from must not happen here); GT Trunks operates in the *present-as-aftermath* (the adult president running Capsule Corporation in the GT-canon adulthood Toei animated); Trunks: Xeno fights to *preserve* canonical history against corruption (the Xenoverse Time Patrol position is structurally identical to the franchise itself defending its own canon from in-game alternative-histories).

The character's specific institutional features across the corpus are:

- **Half-Saiyan / half-Earthling lineage.** Trunks is canonically half-Saiyan (from Vegeta) and half-Earthling (from Bulma), the second canonical half-Saiyan child in the franchise after Gohan (Goku and Chi-Chi's son). The half-Saiyan inheritance is shared across all three incarnations and is the canonical mechanism by which Trunks accesses Super Saiyan transformations (canonically he reaches SS, SS2, and SSG forms across various story arcs). His mother Bulma is canonically the Capsule Corporation president whose engineering work built the time machine that Future Trunks uses to travel from his future-timeline; his father Vegeta is canonically the Prince of All Saiyans whose moral-redemption arc across Z is partly motivated by his son.
- **The Brave Sword.** Future Trunks' canonical weapon across his Z appearances and the *Dragon Ball Super* Future Trunks Saga is the Brave Sword, a broad-bladed weapon that he carries on his back and uses for sword-and-ki combat (rather than the pure-ki-combat operating mode that most Saiyan-lineage fighters in the franchise use). The sword is one of the most canonical Trunks visual identifiers; the Quick blade Trunks SD3-03 print and the Unrelenting Assault Trunks BT3-112 print both depict the Brave Sword (the latter as the Xeno-incarnation continuation of the same weapon). GT Trunks canonically does not use the sword in adult form; the corpus's two GT-era prints (Quick Rush BT3-011, Intensifying Power BT4-012) depict him in unarmed combat poses consistent with his GT-era operating mode.
- **The mother-Bulma / father-Vegeta foil role.** Across his Z, GT, and Xeno incarnations, Trunks functions canonically as Vegeta's moral foil — the half-Saiyan son whose Saiyan inheritance is partially balanced by his Earthling-Bulma upbringing, producing a character who *does not* default to Vegeta's pride-driven combat operating mode and *does* default to a collaborative-with-Earthlings register. The Future Trunks Saga in DBS 2016 explicitly foregrounds this dynamic when adult-Future-Trunks returns to the present and interacts with both his canonical father Vegeta and the Z-warriors who knew him as a baby in his original timeline.
- **The Xenoverse continuity expansion.** The Bandai Namco / Dimps *Dragon Ball Xenoverse* franchise (2015 onward) expanded Future Trunks' canon by giving him a non-Toriyama continuation as a Time Patrol lieutenant under Chronoa's leadership. This expansion is canonical to the Xenoverse video games and the *Super Dragon Ball Heroes* web anime, distinct from Toriyama's Z / DBS continuity. The Xeno incarnation is sometimes referred to as "Trunks: Xeno" or as the "Time Patrol Trunks" in Bandai's official materials. Xeno Trunks is canonically the Time Patrol's principal Trunks-side operative against the Time Breakers ([[xenoverse-villains]]: Demigra, Towa, Mira), making the Xeno-incarnation node-to-node relation a direct canonical adversarial pairing.

This is what BBL's bundle copy can lean on without inventing canon: Trunks is, canonically across Toriyama and Toei and Bandai, the franchise's most-instantiated time-displaced hero, present as three named incarnations in seven Dragon Ball Super Card Game prints in our corpus across two sets (Cross Worlds BT3 + Colossal Warfare BT4 + Mythic Booster MB-01 reprints). A future Trunks-themed Discrete Lair could anchor on the multi-incarnation framing without reaching past canon: the same canonical character rendered into three timeline-relations across three decades of franchise output, with the BBL corpus holding cards across all three.

## Caveats

- **One parent, three incarnations, no premature solo split (yet).** Following the Tamiyo precedent (single character node with multi-aspect roster covering Moon Sage / Field Researcher / Collector of Tales / Inquisitive Student / Compleated Sage incarnations across one anchor) and the Nicol Bolas precedent (single character node with Bolas-Falls cluster + apparatus aspects across one anchor), the canonical move is one parent Trunks node anchoring all three incarnations. Future Trunks alone has 4 corpus cards — past the standard solo-node threshold — but the canonical move is to keep all three incarnations under one parent until the corpus demands a split. A future split could partition the parent into `trunks-future`, `trunks-gt`, `trunks-xeno` solo sub-nodes if the corpus accumulates enough per-incarnation density (e.g., 6+ Future Trunks cards plus 4+ GT Trunks cards plus 3+ Xeno cards) to warrant the split. For now the parent holds; the per-aspect framing in the "Aspects in the corpus" section above does the disambiguation work that a future solo-sub-node split would formalize.

- **Bandai's character-tag field is the canonical per-print incarnation identifier.** Each Dragon Ball Super Card Game card carries an official character tag in Bandai's database that disambiguates which incarnation the print depicts: `Trunks` (incarnation-ambiguous or generic), `Trunks: Future` (the Z-canon / DBS Future Trunks Saga adult incarnation, the most common DBSCG tag), `Trunks: GT` (the Toei GT-era continuation), `Trunks: Xeno` (the Bandai Xenoverse / Super Dragon Ball Heroes incarnation), `Trunks: Kid` (the present-timeline child Trunks). Cards in the corpus' `appears_on` carry the incarnation identifier in their trivia bullets; future Trunks-print acquisitions should preserve the Bandai-canonical tag in the card's trivia per the [[bbl-triviabot-no-role-identity-conflation]] memory rule. Trunks: Xeno specifically is *not* a renamed Future Trunks — it is a continuity-distinct character with a separate canonical history, and conflating the two is a Mr. Nodeley-grade graph error.

- **Visual identifier ambiguity: hair color.** Across the three incarnations the canonical hair color is rendered inconsistently per artist and per arc: Toei's original GT animation uses *purple* for adult-Trunks hair; Toriyama's Dragon Ball Super manga and Toei's DBS anime for the Future Trunks Saga drift the Future Trunks color to *blue-tinted* (the BT3-062 Bridge to the Future print captures this); Xenoverse's Bandai art treats Future-and-Xeno Trunks with *lavender / light purple*. The hair-color visual identifier is NOT reliable on its own for incarnation disambiguation; use the Bandai character tag + the costume signature (brown open jacket = GT; red scarf = DBS Future Trunks Saga; dark jacket without scarf = Xeno or Z-era Future Trunks). Per the [[bbl-anti-confab-prompt]] memory rule's hair-color caution, vision-pass agents should not anchor incarnation identification on hair palette alone.

- **Future Trunks' sword is canonical; GT Trunks' is not.** The Brave Sword is Future Trunks' canonical weapon in the Z manga and across the DBS Future Trunks Saga; it is *not* a GT-era weapon and adult-GT-Trunks canonically does not wield it. The two corpus GT prints (BT3-011 Quick Rush, BT4-012 Intensifying Power) correctly depict him in unarmed combat poses; the Future-and-Xeno prints with sword depictions (SD3-03 Quick blade, BT3-112 Unrelenting Assault) correctly anchor the weapon's incarnation scope. If a future GT-Trunks print depicts a sword, the depiction would either be a designer choice (the DBSCG can deploy non-canonical depictions for set-thematic reasons) or an error; check Bandai's character tag for canonical clarification.

- **Kid Trunks is in corpus.** The current corpus has one Kid Trunks card (TB2-007 Tiny Rivals Trunks, designer-coordinated paired-print with TB2-004 Tiny Rivals Son Goten in the *World Martial Arts Tournament* TB2 promo set's Goten-and-Trunks Junior Tournament pairing) plus one card depicting Future Trunks alongside his father Vegeta (BT2-063 Father-Son Galick Gun, the DBS episode 65 canonical attack). Kid Trunks is the present-timeline child Trunks raised by Bulma and Vegeta on Earth, distinct from the time-traveling adult Future Trunks, and is canonically a fourth named Trunks incarnation alongside Future / GT / Xeno. He appears in *Dragon Ball Z* from the Cell Saga onward and across *Dragon Ball Super*. The Kid Trunks corpus print anchors the **fourth named incarnation** under the same parent — the four-incarnation framing is the canonical full set, and the TB2-007 print is the first instance to land.

- **Sibling continuity-strand nodes apply.** [[xenoverse-villains]] is the direct adversarial relation for the Trunks: Xeno aspect — Xeno Trunks is canonically the Time Patrol's principal Trunks-side operative against the Time Breakers (Demigra, Towa, Mira), so the two nodes' canonical opposition is a load-bearing graph relation. [[baby-dbgt]] is the direct adversarial relation for the GT-Trunks aspect — Baby is the Tuffle-parasite Saga antagonist that the GT Trunks corpus print (BT4-012 Intensifying Power Trunks, era `Baby Saga`) canonically fights. Both relations are encoded in `related_characters:` and surface as cross-edges when a future Trunks-themed bundle reaches Discrete Lair assembly.

- **Charizard-line / Mareep-line precedent for evolution-line nodes does NOT apply.** The Trunks parent node is a *multi-incarnation single character* anchor, not an *evolution line* (the Pokemon evolution-line nodes like [[charizard-line]], [[mareep-line]], [[staraptor-line]] anchor a designer-coordinated species line where each evolution stage is a canonically-distinct species; Trunks is a single character whose three incarnations are not species-distinct, they are *continuity-strand-distinct*). The structural precedent for Trunks is the `tamiyo` / `nicol-bolas` multi-aspect single-character pattern, not the evolution-line pattern.
