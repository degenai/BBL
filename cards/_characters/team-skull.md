# Team Skull

The Alola-region villainous criminal team in *Pokemon Sun and Moon* (Game Freak / Creatures Inc. / Nintendo, 2016) and its sequel pair *Pokemon Ultra Sun and Ultra Moon* (Game Freak, 2017). Headquartered in the abandoned Po Town settlement on Ula'ula Island — a former gated community taken over and graffitied with the gang's skull-and-crossbones tag — Team Skull is led by Boss **Guzma** (Bug-type specialist with a signature Golisopod, the Alola Pokemon League's most prominent Island Challenge failure-to-defeat-a-Kahuna case) and sole Admin **Plumeria** (Poison-type specialist with a signature Salazzle, framed in-canon as the gang's "big sister" caretaker and operational enforcer). The rank-and-file are Skull Grunts — uniformed in black-and-white striped tops, sagging shorts, and skull-tattoo wrist accessories, speaking in the hip-hop-inflected register that is the gang's most-cited surface signature. **What structurally distinguishes Team Skull from prior franchise villainous teams (Rocket, Magma, Aqua, Galactic, Plasma, Flare) is that its membership is canonically framed as Pokemon League rejects**: per Bulbapedia and the in-game dialogue across both version pairs, every Skull Grunt is a young Alolan trainer who attempted the Island Challenge, failed at one of the kahunas' Grand Trials, and joined the gang as the post-failure alternative to legitimate competitive Pokemon participation. Guzma himself is canonically framed as a failed Kahuna trial-attempter — he could not defeat his own region's Kahuna at the Grand Trial and turned to gang leadership as the post-failure path. The post-game story arc reframes Team Skull as junior partners to the Aether Foundation under Lusamine — the gang is *used* by the larger conspiracy and is canonically the weaker, scrappier villain cohort rather than the apex-villain organization the Aether Foundation turns out to be.

## What this node anchors

Team Skull is the BBL corpus's second Pokemon-side criminal-syndicate anchor (after [[team-rocket]]) and the first whose institutional framing is **failed-League-rejection rather than overt-criminal-syndicate-from-foundation**. ## The functional ideology

Team Skull's most BBL-relevant property is that it is the franchise's **first failed-aspiration Villainous Team**. Where Team Rocket and its successor regional cohorts (Magma, Aqua, Galactic, Plasma, Flare) are framed as ideologues or criminal-enterprises-from-foundation — characters joined because they *believed* in the org's mission or wanted criminal profit — Team Skull's membership is, in-canon, post-failure rejected-youth. Every Skull Grunt is a young Alolan trainer who could not complete the Island Challenge. Guzma himself, the Boss, is a failed Kahuna trial-attempter. The gang is structurally framed as **what young trainers do when the Pokemon League rejects them**, and the in-game dialogue is explicit about this: Skull Grunts repeatedly reference their League failures as the reason they joined Skull, and Guzma's late-game dialogue acknowledges his own failure-to-defeat-a-Kahuna as the wound that drove him.

The Skull apparatus's specific institutional features are:

- **Po Town as occupied-abandoned-settlement.** Po Town is a former gated community on Ula'ula Island that Team Skull occupies after its abandonment — graffitied with the gang's tag, repurposed as the gang's living quarters, with Guzma's "boss room" mansion at its center. The headquarters' framing as a *taken-over abandoned wealthy enclave* is structurally unlike Team Rocket's Game Corner basement (criminal-enterprise-disguised-as-business) or Team Galactic's high-tech HQ (corporate-criminal-apparatus). Skull occupies a place the legitimate economy abandoned.
- **Hip-hop-coded uniform aesthetic.** Black-and-white striped tops, oversized shorts, skull-tattoo wrist accessories, beanies, sunglasses, and the gang's tagging-graffiti visual language. The gang's surface signifier is the **young-urban-disaffected register** transposed into the Pokemon universe; per Bulbapedia, the Game Freak designers (led by Junichi Masuda's team) explicitly cited late-1990s and early-2000s urban-youth-gang aesthetics as the design reference. This is the franchise's first Villainous Team whose visual register is generational-subculture-coded rather than uniform-rank-coded the way Rocket's R-emblazoned uniforms are.
- **Junior-partner reveal.** The post-game story arc reveals Team Skull as junior partners to the Aether Foundation under President Lusamine — the gang is *used* as enforcement-and-distraction by the larger conspiracy, and Guzma's late-game arc is the realization that he and his gang were always the apex-villain's pawns. The Sun and Moon storyline's actual antagonist apparatus is the Aether Foundation's corporate-philanthropic cover; Team Skull is the apparent-villain decoy that gets reframed in Act 3.
- **Plumeria as caretaker-Admin.** Plumeria is canonically the gang's "big sister" — enforcing discipline strictly while genuinely caring for the Skull Grunts as social-rejected-youth she has taken responsibility for. This is structurally distinct from any other franchise Villainous Team's Admin tier: Team Rocket's Executives (Archer, Ariana, Petrel, Proton) are ideological careerists; Team Galactic's Commanders (Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Charon) are scientific-mystical fanatics; Team Plasma's Sages are ideological-religious. Plumeria is *social-services-disguised-as-gang-enforcement*, the only Admin-tier role across the franchise framed as protective-of-rank-and-file rather than disciplinary-or-careerist.

This is what BBL's bundle copy can lean on without inventing canon: Team Skull is, in-canon, a designer-confirmed failed-aspiration cohort dressed in hip-hop register, occupying an abandoned wealthy enclave, led by a failure-of-the-Grand-Trial Boss and a caretaker Admin, and ultimately revealed as junior partners to the actual apex villain. Bundle prose that names the Skulls by name earns lore-aware buyer credibility and lets Game Freak's own designer-confirmed reject-the-League framing do the thesis work — Skull is the Pokemon League's *waste product*, narratively positioned as the gang of those whose competitive trainer careers ended at a Kahuna's Grand Trial.

## Caveats

- **Guzma does NOT yet warrant a solo character node.** The corpus has one Guzma depiction (via the Guzma & Hala TAG TEAM print) and no Guzma-solo cards yet (BUS-115 Guzma Supporter is the canonical solo print but is not in inventory). Per the Bolas-cluster precedent (3-card minimum for a solo character node) and the Tamiyo / Jace / Nicol Bolas pattern of single named characters anchoring on ≥3 corpus cards, Guzma is below threshold. The faction-collective node is the right scope for current corpus state. If Guzma-specific cards reach 3 (e.g. a future BUS-115 Guzma Supporter, a future Guzma's Golisopod variant, an alternate-art TAG TEAM Guzma), a sibling `guzma.md` solo node becomes warranted and would `related_characters:` into this node — the same shape as the Giovanni-pending-solo-node case under [[team-rocket]].

- **Plumeria similarly does NOT yet warrant a solo character node.** Corpus has one Plumeria depiction (BUS-120). Threshold is 3. Same pattern: Plumeria's individual depictions accumulate at the faction-collective node until threshold for a solo node is reached.

- **The Guzma & Hala TAG TEAM is a designer-confirmed cross-cohort pairing.** TAG TEAM Supporter cards in the Cosmic Eclipse cycle are not arbitrary character pairings — each card pairs two characters whose relationship is canonically significant. The Guzma + Hala pairing surfaces the *failed-trial-attempter and the Kahuna he failed against* relationship: Hala is canonically the Melemele Island Kahuna whose Grand Trial Guzma failed at, and the TAG TEAM card's visual conceit (the gang boss and the senior authority figure sharing a frame, in similar tropical lighting and composition) makes the failure-and-its-aftermath the unstated narrative subject of the card. This is why the TAG TEAM card anchors two structurally distinct character nodes at once: the card's *narrative subject* is the relationship between the two cohort tiers (League senior vs League-reject gang), not either character individually.

- **Team Skull and the Aether Foundation are adjacent but distinct.** The Sun and Moon storyline's Act 3 reveal positions Team Skull as junior partners to the Aether Foundation under President Lusamine — Aether is the actual apex-villain apparatus, the corporate-philanthropic-cover-for-multiverse-extraction org, structurally the Alola-era parallel to Macro Cosmos under Chairman Rose in Galar (sibling to [[galar-gym-challenge]]). When Aether Foundation cards reach the corpus, they belong to a sibling `aether-foundation` character node (not this one); a future Lusamine character node would `related_characters:` to both this node (her Team Skull junior-partner relationship) and the new Aether node. The two-nodes-not-one decision predicates on Aether's apex-villain status — Team Skull is canonically *used* by Aether but is not Aether.

- **The hip-hop-coded uniform aesthetic is designer-confirmed, not editorial reading.** Bulbapedia documents the Game Freak design team's explicit citation of late-1990s and early-2000s urban-youth-gang aesthetics as the visual reference for Team Skull. Bundle copy can lean on this framing as canonical designer intent rather than as audience reading. This distinguishes Team Skull's visual register from Team Rocket's uniform-rank-coded R-emblazoned suits, which derive from a different design-reference lineage (Cold-War-era criminal-syndicate iconography). The two villainous-team-iconography registers — uniform-rank-coded vs subculture-coded — are themselves a designer-confirmed franchise-evolution beat that bundle copy can index.

- **Modeled as a character-layer node, not a hub.** Like `team-rocket` (Kanto villain-team), `alola-elite-four` (Alola Pokemon League institutional cohort), `galar-gym-challenge` (Galar Pokemon League institutional cohort), `endriders` (Aetherdrift racing-team collective), `orzhov-syndicate` / `azorius-senate` (Ravnica faction-institutions), this is a faction-collective entity hosted on the character layer per the Endriders precedent. Single character layer, same precedent.
