# Tsukumogami

A foundational `Bulk Graph Bundler` hub. Like [[chinese-zodiac]], it is a cultural framework rather than a political-economy verb — it reads bulk through a lens that is defensibly folklore-and-design-history rather than "themed boosters."

The subject is plain: **the animate object.** The inert thing that has a life. Not allegory — objects are objects. Tsukumogami (付喪神), "tool kami," is the Japanese folkloric name for the oldest unease anyone has ever had in a house full of possessions: that the things you own are not as inert as they look.

> Two trading-card universes, decades apart and an ocean apart, kept drawing the same picture: a teacup, a phone, a sword, a lantern — and the question *what if it woke up.* BBL did not invent this category. Game Freak and Wizards both kept returning to it. This hub is where that recurring picture gets one home instead of a hundred loose card-to-card edges.

## The Folklore, Stated Straight

The hub uses a real word, so it carries the real scholarship — anything less would be using the prestige of a foreign term to dignify generic ghost-gadgets.

Per the *Tsukumogami-ki emaki*, a tool that has served its owners for **one hundred years** receives a kami and becomes a tsukumogami — animate, aware, and possessed of its own accrued spirit. The folklore's load-bearing motif is **the discard**: in the *Tsukumogami-ki* tale, abandoned utensils convene after their hundredth year and plot revenge on the owners who threw them out, before a monk leads them to Buddhist salvation. The custom of *susu-harai* — year-end house-cleaning — was in part a practice of discarding objects *before* they reached a hundred years, so they could not wake.

The decisive structural point, and the hub's organizing distinction: a tsukumogami is **the object's own latent spirit awakening from within.** This is not the same as an object *possessed* by an external entity, and not the same as a *constructed* automaton animated by a maker. Those are different folk-mechanisms and the hub keeps them named apart.

One honesty note carried from the research: neither franchise honors the strict hundred-year rule. Pokémon's Rotom is Bulbapedia-cited as tsukumogami-based and yet inhabits a washing machine and a smartphone; the rule survives in the franchise only as an Easter egg — Voltorb is National Pokédex **No. 100**, the threshold encoded as a joke about itself. The hub therefore reserves the term **true tsukumogami** for the cases that genuinely fit, and is plain that the rest is looser.

## What This Hub Anchors

Two registers, and the distinction between them is the hub — not a gradient, a clean cut.

**True / conventional / traditional tsukumogami.** The genuine folkloric case: an aged everyday object whose *own* spirit awakens from within. In corpus, this is the **Sinistea / Polteageist** line (the antique teacup and teapot — canonical Pokédex flavor: *"born when a lonely spirit possessed a cold, leftover cup of tea"*), **Voltorb** (the Poké Ball that achieved tsukumogami-like awakening, Pokédex No. 100 encoding the hundred-year threshold), and the **Honedge → Doublade → Aegislash** sword-spirit line (the *tsurugi*-becoming-aware tradition, one of Japan's oldest tsukumogami categories). On the *Magic* side the true case is canonically **Kamigawa** — the plane Wizards built on the premise that "there are kami for waterfalls and kami for umbrellas"; **Lantern Kami** is the textbook card, the spirit of a paper lantern walking. Kamigawa is not yet in the BBL corpus, but the canonical legitimacy is on record for when it enters stock.

**Tsukumogami-adjacent.** Everything else gathered here, named honestly as *not* the true thing:
- **Modern animate objects** — the object is alive but it is a modern manufactured thing, not a century-aged one. Rotom's appliance forms, the Klink gear-line, Duskmourn's Living Phone, the *Throne of Eldraine* fairy-tale objects (Sorcerer's Broom, Inquisitive Puppet, Clockwork Servant). The picture is tsukumogami; the object's age is not.
- **Externally-possessed objects** — and these belong to a *different tradition entirely.* Duskmourn's objects are puppeted by the demon Valgavoth: a soul ripped and stapled on from outside, not earned from within. That is the **Western haunted-object tradition** — the poltergeist, the Annabelle / Chucky / killer-doll register — and the hub names it as a distinct sibling folklore standing in contrast, never silently absorbed into the Japanese concept. The [[dsk-toy-horror]] cohort is the corpus's home for this register.

## Narrative Seeds (lair titles, not yet built)

- **"A Hundred Years of Service"** — the true-tsukumogami lair. Sinistea / Polteageist + Voltorb + the Honedge line. The genuine folkloric core, copy leaning on the cross-generational design-source continuity.
- **"The House Was Awake"** — the externally-possessed contrast lair. Duskmourn's Living Phone, Malevolent Chandelier, Haunted Screen, Ragged Playmate. Copy names the register honestly: Western haunted-object horror, the poltergeist, not tsukumogami.
- **"It Volunteered for the Washing Machine"** — the modern-animate-object lair. Rotom's appliance forms, the Klink gears, the Eldraine fairy-tale objects. The thesis is the franchises' own loose usage — the picture without the hundred years.
- **"Two Ways to Wake a Thing"** — the comparative cross-IP lair. True tsukumogami beside the Western puppet: the object that earned a soul next to the object that had one forced into it. The seam *is* the bundle.

## Anti-Patterns

This hub is NOT:
- **Generic "spooky object" aesthetic.** A vaguely eerie item with no awakening — no spirit, no animation, no folk-mechanism — is the Spirit Halloween aisle, not this hub.
- **Golems and constructs.** A thing *built* by a maker to be animate from its first moment was never an everyday object that woke up. Generic golems / constructs are external-fabrication, structurally outside tsukumogami; exclude them unless a specific card's lore frames the construct as an object's own emergent spirit.
- **The Japanese concept flattened onto Western horror.** The hub holds both the tsukumogami and the poltergeist registers, but flattening one into the other — letting "tsukumogami" quietly mean "any haunted object" — is the precise failure to avoid. Keep them named apart in every bundle's copy.
- **A creature wearing or wielding an object.** Mimikyu wears a costume; a knight holds a sword. A creature with a prop is not an animate object. The object itself must be the one that is alive.

## Design Note

This hub is **deliberately card-edge disconnected** in Obsidian's graph view. The node is visible in the layer-node clique — linked to other layer nodes via body wikilinks — but **no edges connect this hub to any individual card.** The wire flows hub → character-cohort node → card, never hub → card.

Routing is to two existing character-layer cohort nodes:
- [[tsukumogami-pokemon]] — the Pokémon object-becomes-spirit design-source cohort (Voltorb, Sinistea / Polteageist, Honedge line, Shuppet). Holds the franchise's true-tsukumogami cases.
- [[dsk-toy-horror]] — the *Duskmourn* corrupted-childhood artifact-creature cohort. Holds the externally-possessed Western-horror register.

The aged / modern / externally-possessed *texture* lives down in those cohort nodes, where the `tsukumogami-pokemon` node already codifies it. The hub does not inherit the mess: the hub is one clean concept, the cohorts carry the roster. There is no `appears_on:` or `anchored_cards:` field on this node and there will not be — cards are inventory, bundles are destructive, and `anchored_lairs:` is the only card-adjacent relationship that survives the inventory churn (see `bbl-bundles-are-destructive-on-graph.md`).

An Eldraine fairy-tale-animate-object cohort node is a natural future third routing target, once those cards are enriched and a cohort node is commissioned for them.
