## Trivia

- **Set context** — Klang is National Pokedex no. 600, the Stage 1 mid-form of the Klink evolution line: Klink (no. 599, evolves at level 38 into Klang) -> Klang (no. 600) -> Klinklang (no. 601, evolves at level 49). Introduced in Generation 5 (Unova) in Pokemon Black and White (2010/2011), the Klink line was among the first Pokemon designed as purely mechanical entities rather than animals with metallic traits. `[pokemondb.net: Klang Pokedex entry; Bulbapedia: Klang, via WebSearch snippet]`
- **Flavor text** — "When Klang goes all out, the minigear links up perfectly with the outer part of the big gear, and this Pokemon's rotation speed increases sharply." `[pokemon.com TCG database: Darkness Ablaze 126]` The text mechanically describes the same gear-linkage visible in the art: the smaller minigear aligned with the larger gear's outer ring, framing mechanical efficiency as an in-battle power escalation.
- **Design** — Darkness Ablaze no. 126/189, Stage 1, Steel-type, 90 HP, Uncommon, illustrated by NC Empire (released 2020-08-14). NC Empire is a studio illustrator with a large SWSH-era catalog; credited across multiple Steel-type and mechanical-Pokemon cards. Darkness Ablaze (SWSH3) is themed around Charizard VMAX and Eternatus VMAX as its marquee pulls — Klang is an uncommon Steel-type filler. `[pokemon.com TCG database; Serebii: NC Empire artist index, via WebSearch snippet]`

### Second-pass trivia (Discrete Lair 002 Drone deep harvest)

- **Designer origin** — Klink was designed by Ken Sugimori after an internal Game Freak brief for "something inorganic." Sugimori gravitated toward gears because the concept gave the Pokemon an expressive face — a design priority he has cited repeatedly — and noted that Klink's face shifts as its gears turn direction. The Klink line was explicitly modeled on the Magneton / Dugtrio pattern: multiples of a first stage stacked to produce a line, rather than a morphological transformation. `[Lava Cut Content: "Gen 5 Historia: Pokemon Origin Stories (Part 4)," translating Nintendo Dream magazine (2011) — lavacutcontent.com/ken-sugimori-nintendo-dream-6/]`

- **"Object Pokemon" controversy** — Community sentiment on the Klink line, particularly during 2011-2013, positioned it alongside the Vanillite line as the Gen 5 "object Pokemon" poster children: designs accused of lazy literalism (gear = Pokemon). The line-specific criticism held that each stage was incremental gear-accumulation rather than genuine metamorphosis, with Klinklang described on Smogon and PokeCommunity boards as feeling more like a "power-up" than an evolved creature. The discourse cooled by the Sword and Shield era but never fully settled; retrospective articles have revisited it as a proxy debate about what counts as a valid Pokemon concept. `[Community sentiment — PokeCommunity Forums: "The Trubbish line and the Vanillite line: Why all the hate?"; Smogon Forums: "Let's talk about Pokemon designs"]`

- **NC Empire — studio profile** — NC Empire describes itself as an "Association for Design and Illustration" and began its Pokemon TCG contributions during the Sun and Moon era Promos. As of 2026 they have 73+ card credits in the TCG, covering Pokemon, Trainer, and promotional product artwork. Outside the Pokemon TCG, they hold the official BEYBLADE X logo design credit — a detail that marks them as a commercial illustration studio with crossover franchise work rather than a Pokemon-exclusive art house. `[Bulbapedia: NC Empire; PocketMonsters.Net: NC Empire biography; Art of PKM: artofpkm.com/illustrators/122]`

- **Competitive legacy — Plasma Steel Klinklang** — Klinklang (Plasma Storm, 2013) — the final evolution of this line — anchored one of the Black and White era's most tactically unusual decks. Its Plasma Steel ability prevented all damage to the player's Metal-type Pokemon from opponent's Pokemon-EX, creating a blanket EX-immunity shell at a time when EX attackers dominated the format. Michael Slutsky piloted the build to Top 32 at 2013 US Nationals, going undefeated into the T64 bracket, pairing Klinklang's ability with Max Potion healing and Cobalion as primary attacker. SixPrizes characterized it as an "off the wall" list that forced the EX-heavy meta to find non-EX answers. `[SixPrizes: "The Strategy Behind Plasma Klinklang" (2013-03-22); SixPrizes: "Heavy Metal: A Top 32 US Nationals Report and a Look at Klinklang" (2013-07-13)]`

- **Pokedex — the death entry** — Across Klang's game Pokedex entries, the Black 2 / White 2 and Pokemon Y text reads: "A minigear and big gear comprise its body. If the minigear it launches at a foe doesn't return, it will die." The line frames the detachable minigear as both Klang's primary weapon and its existential anchor — launching it is an act of total commitment, and its non-return is lethal. The Darkness Ablaze TCG flavor text ("when Klang goes all out, the minigear links up perfectly with the outer part of the big gear") describes the opposite pole: peak gear coherence as maximum performance. Both entries describe the same mechanism from opposite angles: engagement and risk. `[Pokemon Database: Klang — pokemondb.net/pokedex/klang; Bulbapedia: Klang]`

- **Anime — BW063 and the pen-in-the-gears** — The Klink line's most distinct anime appearance is episode BW063, "Evolution Exchange Excitement!" (Season 15, Episode 15 in the US broadcast), set in Chargestone Cave. The episode features a Klinklang that has gone haywire because a pen was accidentally lodged in its gears by Sunglasses Krokorok in an earlier episode, causing uncontrolled electrical discharge until Professor Juniper removes it. Klink and Klang also appear in the theatrical films "White — Victini and Zekrom" and "Black — Victini and Reshiram" under the ownership of a character named Mannes. `[Bulbapedia: BW063; Serebii: Episode 724 — serebii.net/anime/epiguide/bestwishes/724.shtml; Bulbapedia: Klang]`
