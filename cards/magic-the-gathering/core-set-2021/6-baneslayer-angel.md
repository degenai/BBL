## Trivia

- **Set context / design history** — Baneslayer Angel was first printed in Magic 2010 (M10, July 2009) and is reprinted here in M21. Its M10 debut made it the defining chase mythic of that era: a 5/5 with flying, first strike, lifelink, and protection from Demons and Dragons for {3}{W}{W} that reached approximately $50 on the secondary market — an unprecedented price for a Standard-legal mythic from a core set. The M21 printing is its eighth overall, including a 2015 appearance in From the Vault: Angels. All printings use Greg Staples's original art. `[Scryfall; CoolStuffInc "Baneslayer Angel: The Perfect Storm"; Quiet Speculation, December 2009 — via WebSearch]`
- **Art history** — According to Cook and Becker's fine-art print catalogue, the Baneslayer Angel painting was originally created for Serra Angel. Wizards deemed it too ethereal for Serra's established look; Staples repainted Serra Angel and the original composition became Baneslayer. The original oil painting was sold in December 2020 by Star City Games President Pete Hoefling for $50,000, selling within ten minutes of listing. Staples also produced a signed lithograph "Angel Series" featuring the card, sold through Star City Games. `[Cook and Becker gallery listing via WebSearch; Hipsters of the Coast, "SCG President Pete Hoefling Sells $160k in Original MTG Angel Paintings," December 2020]`
- **Flavor text** — "Some angels protect the meek and innocent. Others seek out and smite evil wherever it lurks." No speaker attributed. The text encodes the card's design identity: Baneslayer is a hunter-archetype angel with specific prey (Demons and Dragons), not a guardian. `[Scryfall]`
- **Mechanical reputation** — EDHREC rank 4,768; sustained Commander adoption as a white finisher and lifegain-combo piece. Dominant in M10 Standard (2009-2010), later eclipsed as threats scaled. Not Pauper-legal (mythic rarity). Legal in Pioneer, Modern, Legacy, Vintage, Commander. `[Scryfall]`
- **Community resonance** — Community sentiment (Pojo.com "MTG Throwback Thursday"; r/magicTCG historical discussion): Baneslayer is widely cited as the card that proved core-set mythics could be financially explosive, establishing the template for what the community now calls a "chase mythic." Its collector-market presence — high-end reproductions through Cook and Becker and INPRNT — reflects recognition beyond gameplay value. `[Pojo.com via WebSearch; Cook and Becker/INPRNT via WebSearch — sentiment]`
