# Disguise (mechanic)

The iconographic-and-rules-text primitive of the **Disguise** keyword introduced in *Murders at Karlov Manor* (MKM, 2024-02-09) as one of the set's four signature mechanics (alongside Suspect, Cases, and Collect Evidence). Disguise is a designer-coordinated alternative-cost keyword that lets a creature card be cast face down for `{3}` as a 2/2 with ward `{2}`, then flipped face up at any time for its Disguise cost (which appears as `Disguise {cost}` in the oracle text). The mechanic renders the in-fiction *act of going undercover* as a rules-text-level state shift: the operative deploys cheap and anonymous behind ward-armor, then reveals identity (and full ability stack) only when the right moment arrives. The marker is a first-class graph-relevant mechanical primitive parallel to [[suspect]] and [[manifest-dread]]: a designer-coordinated cohort identifier that crosses multiple cards in a set, marks them as members of a designer-stated thematic program, and provides the canonical iconographic anchor for any bundle assembled around the program.

## The functional ideology

The Disguise mechanic is the canonical-by-printing mark for the MKM **undercover-operative mechanical layer**. Per Mark Rosewater's MKM mechanic preview articles (January 2024) and the *Murders at Karlov Manor* Release Notes (2024-02-01), Disguise is one of MKM's four signature mechanics, designed as the in-fiction equivalent of *deploying a creature under cover*: the player commits to a face-down 2/2 with ward `{2}` for a flat `{3}` cost, and the creature's true identity (and its disguise-cost flip cost) stays private until the controller reveals it by paying the cost. Disguise is functionally a Wizards-renamed-and-warded variant of the older Morph mechanic (Onslaught, 2002) — the action word "Disguise" replaces "Morph" to lean into the set's noir-procedural register, and the +ward `{2}` rider adds protection against the targeted-removal answers that classic Morph had no counter for. Per Rosewater's "Top of the Murderpalooza" preview articles, Disguise was paired with the new keyword action *Cloak* (which puts non-Disguise cards face down as 2/2s with ward `{2}` but without the ability to be flipped face up) to support the set's investigation-by-revelation rhythm.

The cohort's most BBL-relevant property is its **direct dual-coding of undercover-operative work as combat-rules**. Where [[suspect]] renders the in-fiction *status of being investigated* as a creature condition, and Cases render the *open investigation* as enchantment-with-solve-trigger, Disguise renders the in-fiction *act of operating under a false identity* as the face-down play itself. The three mechanics interlock: a Disguised creature can be Suspected when turned face up if a controller-targeted Case is solved or an opposing card forces the flip; a Cloaked creature (from Expose the Culprit's exile-and-cloak mode) cannot itself be Disguise-flipped but plays the same hidden-deployment register. The whole MKM gameplay rendering is the procedural layer Wizards designed to make detective work *feel* like detective work at the rules-text level, and Disguise carries the *operative-side* half of that procedural layer (Suspect carries the *suspect-status* half).

For any BBL bundle on MKM mechanical cohesion, the undercover-operative register, or the cross-cycle face-down-creature theme, the Disguise mechanic is the highest-leverage iconographic-mechanical anchor available in the *Murders at Karlov Manor* slice of the corpus. Bundle prose that names the mechanic by name earns lore-aware buyer credibility AND lets Wizards' actual designer-coordinated cohort work do the thesis work — Disguise is *literally* the rules-text rendering of operating under a false name, and the storefront copy can lean on that without inventing the angle.

## Caveats

- **Disguise is paired with — but distinct from — Suspect, Cases, Collect Evidence, and the keyword action Cloak.** The four MKM signature mechanics were designed as an interlocking suite per Rosewater's preview articles. Disguise renders the *act* of going undercover (the face-down deployment); [[suspect]] renders the *status* of being investigated (the marker on creatures); Cases render the *open investigation* (enchantment-with-solve-trigger); Collect Evidence renders the *forensic accumulation* (the mill-then-exile cost). Cloak is the lower-cost-no-flip cousin of Disguise, used by cards like Expose the Culprit to put non-Disguise cards face down without the option to ever flip them up. Each warrants its own future symbol-layer node when corpus accumulates ≥3 anchors per mechanic — current Disguise coverage at 21 anchors is by far the largest of the four; Suspect at 5; Cases coverage is at ~6 corpus members; Collect Evidence is at ~2. The Disguise + Suspect pair completes the *creature-side* half of MKM's procedural-mechanics layer; Cases + Collect Evidence completes the *evidence-side* half.
- **The Disguise *keyword* and the *face-down 2/2 with ward 2 creature-state* are co-extensive but conceptually distinct.** Disguise is the alternative-cost keyword the card carries ("Disguise {cost}"); the face-down 2/2 with ward `{2}` is the creature state the affected card occupies after Disguise is paid. Both predicates pick out the same set of cards in current MTG canon, but the **keyword is the symbol-layer anchor** that the BBL graph hosts at the `_symbols/` level. State-tracking is captured in card oracle_text ("Disguise"-containing strings); keyword membership is captured here.
- **Disguise is the renamed warded variant of the classic Morph mechanic.** The original Morph debuted in Onslaught (October 2002) and asked the player to cast a creature face down for `{3}` as a 2/2 with no ward, then flip it face up for its morph cost. Disguise adds the ward `{2}` rider, addressing the targeted-removal weakness of classic Morph creatures, and renames the action word to fit the noir-procedural register. Per Rosewater's preview articles and MTG Wiki's Disguise page, the rename was a deliberate flavor decision rather than a rules change — the mechanic *is* Morph for almost all gameplay purposes, with ward `{2}` as the meaningful rules addition. Future MTG sets may reintroduce Disguise as a returning mechanic or revive classic Morph; per Wizards' mechanic-recycling pattern, the action word may continue to shift while the underlying face-down-creature primitive persists.
- **The mechanic's persistence is set-bounded and unconfirmed-for-future-sets.** *Murders at Karlov Manor* introduced Disguise as a set-specific mechanic; *Outlaws of Thunder Junction* (April 2024) immediate follow-up set did NOT continue the mechanic; *Foundations* and subsequent Standard sets have not (as of 2026-05-14) revived it. The mechanic is canonical for the MKM-and-reprints window.
- **The keyword does NOT have a distinct iconographic graphic device on the card-art level.** Unlike the [[orzhov-signet]] (a graphic device on card art) or the [[single-strike-emblem]] (a graphic device on card art), Disguise does NOT have a visual emblem the artist paints onto the canvas. The "iconography" for this node is the rules-text *appearance of the keyword "Disguise"* in oracle_text rather than a visual device. This matches the [[suspect]] and [[manifest-dread]] precedent: where Orzhov Signet and Single Strike Emblem are *visual* primitives, Disguise is a *mechanical-keyword* primitive. The symbol layer accommodates both; the node-type predicate is *first-class graph-relevant designer-coordinated cohort primitive*, not specifically *visual primitive*.
- **The [[agency]] character-node is the natural cross-edge.** The Disguise-coded corpus anchors are heavily Agency-operational-register cards (Mistway Spy, Faerie Snoop, Gadget Technician, Sanguine Savior, Dog Walker all read as Agency operatives or Agency-adjacent operatives going undercover; the green-side Disguise cards like Nervous Gardener and Greenbelt Radical read as criminal-side undercover work). The mechanic's gameplay loop maps the Agency's undercover-operative procedure (and its anti-Agency mirror) at the rules-text scale. The `related_characters: [agency]` frontmatter field on this node encodes the cross-edge; the Agency node may want a reciprocal `related_symbols: [disguise]` update as a downstream sync task.
